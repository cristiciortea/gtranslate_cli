“Az me dartseylt a mayse a poyer lakht er dray mol. Dem ershtn mol lakht er, ven men dertseylt'em di mayse; dem tsveytn mol ven men darklert'em di mayse; un der dritn mol ven er farshteyt di mayse.”

As men there-tell a joke a peasant laughs he three time, the first-en time laughs he, when men there-tell'im the joke; the two-en time when men there-clears'im the joke; and the third-en time when he understands the joke.

“A porets lakht tsvey mol. Eyn mol lakht er, ven men dartseylt'em un a tsveytn mol, ven me darklert'em, vorem farshteyn farshteyt er say-vi-say nit.”

A noble laughs two time. One time laughs he, when men there-tell'im and a two-en time, when men there-clear'im, for understanding-about understands he be-like-be not.

“An ofitser lakht nor eyn mol, b'shas me dartseylt em, vorem darklern losst er zakh nit un farshteyn farshteyt er nit.”

An officer laughs only one time, at-time men there-tell'im, for there-clear-to lets he self not and understanding-about understands he not.

“A yid, az me dartselyt'em a mayse, makht er "Veys ikh vos? Alte mayses" un er ken der mayse besser dartseyln.”

A Jew, as men there-tell'im a joke, does he "know I what?" Old jokes" and he can the joke better tell-to.
